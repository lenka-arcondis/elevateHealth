How this tool works

What it does

The CVD Population Health Impact Simulator estimates how many deaths and life-years a cardiovascular intervention can prevent in a defined population. You set the size and profile of the group, choose a time horizon, and specify how much the intervention reduces CVD mortality. The tool instantly shows the difference between a world with and without the intervention.

The simulation model

The calculator runs a cohort simulation: it follows a group of people who all start at the same age, and in each annual "cycle" applies age-specific death rates to calculate how many survive to the next year. This is the same fundamental approach used in formal health economic evaluations (Markov cohort models). Two arms run in parallel — one with the intervention, one without — so every output is a direct comparison.

Data sources

  • Mortality rates come from the Human Mortality Database (HMD), the internationally recognised standard for life table data. We use 2023 period life tables for Sweden and Finland, with separate rates for men and women blended according to your % Female input.
  • CVD event rates are based on ischaemic heart disease (IHD) incidence from the Swedish disease registry. These rates are used as a proxy for both countries. A 10% case fatality rate is applied to estimate how many events result in death.

What each output means

  • CVD Events (Baseline): The number of heart disease events expected in the untreated group over the chosen time horizon — giving you a sense of the scale of the problem.
  • Deaths Avoided: The difference in total deaths between the untreated and treated groups. This is the primary measure of the intervention's mortality benefit.
  • Life-years Preserved: The total extra years of life gained across everyone in the population. If 1,000 people each live one extra year, that is 1,000 life-years preserved.
  • Life Expectancy Gain: The average extra time each person gains — expressed in years or months depending on the size of the effect.
  • Micro — Individual survival gain: The improvement in the probability that a single person survives to the end of the time horizon. This is the most personally meaningful number for a patient or clinician.
  • Macro — Population life-years: The total life-years preserved at population scale — the number most relevant to payers and public health decision-makers.

Inputs explained

  • Country: Selects which mortality rates are used. Finnish men have higher cardiovascular mortality than Swedish men at the same age.
  • Population size: The number of people in the simulated group. Larger populations produce bigger absolute numbers, but per-person gains stay the same.
  • Age band: The age at which the cohort starts. CVD risk rises sharply with age, so older cohorts see larger absolute benefits from the same relative reduction.
  • % Female: Blends male and female mortality rates. Women have lower CVD mortality than men at the same age.
  • Time horizon: How many years the simulation runs. Longer horizons accumulate more benefit.
  • CVD Mortality Reduction: The intervention's effect — a 20% reduction means 20 fewer CVD deaths for every 100 that would have occurred without treatment.
Limitations: The intervention effect is applied uniformly across all ages and sexes. CVD incidence data is from 1997 and does not reflect current rates, which have declined significantly. The model does not account for competing risks, comorbidities, or treatment drop-off. All outputs are for illustrative and research purposes only and should not be used for clinical or policy decisions without independent validation.
